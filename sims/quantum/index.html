Quantum Cryptography (BB84)

Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

This simulation follows the BB84 Protocol. In the classroom activity, you used Quantum Coins and Oriented Boxes to share a secret key. Here, the photons represent those coins!

Step 1: Alice Sends

In the Activity: Alice picks a coin (0/1) and a box shape (+ or x). She sends the box to Bob.

The Theory: Alice prepares a quantum state (photon polarization) in a specific basis.

Step 2: Bob Measures

In the Activity: Bob picks a box shape to open Alice's gift. If he picks the wrong box, he "jiggles" the coin, changing its bit!

The Theory: Bob measures the photon in a random basis. If the basis is different, the result is random (50/50).

Step 3: Sifting (The Public Channel)

In the Activity: Alice and Bob shout out their box shapes over the public channel. They never shout the coin bit.

The Theory: They compare bases. They only keep bits where they used the same basis (+ or x).

Step 4: Shared Key

In the Activity: The bits they kept should match perfectly. This is their secret key!

The Theory: Quantum mechanics ensures that if an eavesdropper (Eve) tried to look, she would have "jiggled" the coins, causing detectable errors.

Quantum Coin Analogy

Imagine a gold Quantum Coin inside a glowing box. The box has two slots: a Plus Slot (+) and an X Slot (x).

If you look through the right slot, you see the coin as it is. If you look through the "wrong" slot, you disturb the coin and it flips randomly!

How Polarizers Work

When Bob uses the same basis as Alice, the polarizer acts like a perfect gate. The bit comes through unchanged.

When the bases mismatch, the photon must "choose" a new orientation to pass through. This adds quantum noise!
