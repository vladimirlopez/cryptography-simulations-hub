The Caesar Shift

One of the simplest and most widely known encryption techniques.

History

Named after Julius Caesar, who used it in his private correspondence, the Caesar Shift is a type of substitution cipher. Caesar would shift each letter of his message by a fixed number of positions down the alphabet. For example, with a shift of 3, 'A' would be replaced by 'D', 'B' would become 'E', and so on.

How it Works

The encryption can be represented using modular arithmetic by first transforming the letters into numbers, according to the scheme, A = 0, B = 1, ..., Z = 25.

Encryption of a letter x by a shift n can be described mathematically as:

E_n(x) = (x + n) mod 26

Decryption is performed similarly:

D_n(x) = (x - n) mod 26

The Wheel

To make this process easier, cipher discs or "Caesar Wheels" were invented. These consist of two rotating disks with the alphabet written on them. By aligning the inner disk relative to the outer disk, one can quickly encrypt and decrypt messages without doing mental math.
